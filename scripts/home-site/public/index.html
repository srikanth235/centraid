Centraid · personal software

Software for a market of one.

Most software is built for everyone, which is why none of it quite fits you — and why your life ends up scattered across a hundred vendors' databases. Centraid inverts the ownership: your data lives in one vault on your own machine, on one shared model with no silos; your apps are thin projections over it, written and rewritten by an agent to fit you; your devices connect peer-to-peer to one gateway you run. No accounts, no cloud, no landlord.

How it worksin five chapters

01 your data

One vault holds the whole record.

Your people, money, health, documents, messages and plans live in one vault on your disk, on one shared ontology: "a person", "money moved", "something was measured" are declared exactly once, and every app extends or references them — never re-declares them. Star a document in one app and every other app knows, because there is no silo to copy across.

Apps borrow the record through scoped grants you sign, every access leaves a receipt, and secrets are sealed even at rest. Export is honest: copy a directory.

One consent-checked door, an append-only journal beside it. The data pillar → · the full ontology →

02 your apps

Apps are windows, not warehouses.

Eight blueprints ship in the box — Docs, Photos, Notes, People, Locker, Tally, Agenda, Tasks — and none of them owns a byte of your data. An app is a folder of plain HTML and JavaScript that renders vault rows and mutates them through typed commands. Delete one and nothing of yours is lost.

Don't like how one works? Tell the builder. It edits the app in conversation against a live preview, on a draft — publish flips it onto every device you own. Software that fits, because it's refitted.

No private databases, no build step — a folder you can read in an evening. The apps pillar →

03 the assistant

Data that's alive, not just stored.

The point of one model is that something can finally think across it. The assistant reads your whole vault with plain SQL — receipted, row-capped — and answers questions no siloed app could: joins across your people, money, calendar and documents, because they were never separated.

It writes only through the same typed commands your apps use — consequential acts pause for your confirmation, and when you correct it, the correction becomes a durable rule it consults next time.

Three tools, no side doors: read with SQL, act with commands, open a document. The assistant →

04 the long game

Automations ferry your life back home.

Agents on schedules and data triggers pull your world into the vault — Gmail, calendars, contacts, GitHub — through your own API credentials, with every imported row staged for review and stamped with where it came from. Drop an .mbox, .ics, .vcf or CSV on the import door and it enters the same way.

Writes to the outside world go through an outbox you approve — you read the actual email before it sends. And every run keeps a durable transcript: when you wonder why it did what it did, you read the conversation back.

Cron, webhook, condition, or a change in your data — templates to start from. Automations → · connections →

05 your devices

One gateway. Everything else is a client.

There is no sync service because there is nothing to sync: one gateway holds the vault, and your laptop and phone are clients in a star around it, connected by direct, end-to-end-encrypted peer-to-peer links (iroh) — no relay to rent, no account to lose.

Pairing a phone is one QR code — a ticket that burns on use. Devices authenticate by key, are enrolled per-vault, and revocation cuts live connections, not just future ones. Your agent runtime is pluggable too: Claude Code, Codex, or OpenClaw behind the same leash.

Where it runsone gateway · three homes

It runs where your life runs.

local-first: the vault is files on your machine, the gateway is the only door to it, and nothing leaves your devices unless you point it somewhere.

home 1

Desktop shell

The gateway embedded in an app on your Mac, Linux, or Windows machine. Open it like anything else.

home 2

Always-on daemon

centraid-gateway serve on a spare machine or home server — your apps and automations keep working with the laptop closed.

home 3

Phone in your pocket

The mobile companion is a thin client onto any gateway you run. Same apps, same data, no sync service.

→ get started

One clone to your own vault.

# macOS · Linux · Windows — needs Bun ≥ 1.3 and Node ≥ 24
$ git clone https://github.com/srikanth235/centraid
$ cd centraid && bun install
$ bun run dev:desktop
# a spare machine or home server — devices pair to this
$ bun run build
$ centraid-gateway serve --data-dir ~/centraid-data
$ centraid-gateway pair --data-dir ~/centraid-data --vault Local

First launch asks your name, mints your vault, and lands on the app grid — clone a blueprint from Discover and it's yours to reshape.

Ten minutes in you can pair your phone from Settings → Phone, drop an .mbox or .ics on the import door, and ask the assistant your first cross-silo question. The start guide walks the whole first day.
