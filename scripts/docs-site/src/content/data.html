Chapter 1 · data

One vault. One model. Zero silos.

Everything in Centraid stands on a single claim: the canonical record of your life lives in one place you own, on one shared model, behind one consent-checked door. This chapter walks that claim end to end — the files on disk, the ontology that kills the silos, the consent machinery that makes borrowing possible, and the systems that move data in (connections, automations) and make it useful (the assistant, search).

§01 · the vault

A directory you can copy out.

A vault is not a service. It is a directory of SQLite files on a machine you control, and the whole sovereignty story reduces to what's in it:

File What it holds Why it's separate
vault.db Every schema of the ontology — canonical core, consent plane, agent plane, sync spine, and all life domains — in one ACID boundary. One transaction commits provenance, core rows and domain rows together; all foreign keys stay engine-enforced.
journal.db The append-only audit stream — receipts, provenance, command invocations, checks, evidence — plus the conversation-and-run ledger (chat, automation and builder transcripts). Both bands grow orders of magnitude faster than the model and never roll back a domain write — keeping them out keeps the sovereign asset small.
blobs/ Content-addressed bytes — originals, thumbnails, previews — keyed by sha256. Bytes are rented, meaning is owned: the rows can re-home the bytes to any disk or S3 bucket without touching a relationship.
keys/ (sibling, outside the vault dir) The vault's sealing key (DEK) for encrypted columns. Deliberately outside backup scope — a copied vault can't reveal its own secrets. Custody is yours: centraid-gateway key export / restore / rotate.

A gateway can mount many vaults — each a subdirectory with its own pair of databases, its own identity row, its own grants. Devices are enrolled per-vault, and every request addresses a (gateway, vault) pair explicitly. Export is honest: copy the directory, verify hashes, done.

Only journal.db grows without a natural ceiling, and it's kept bounded without ever dropping history. Idle conversations are digested, their turn-ranges serialized into sealed gzip segments and archived to the same content-addressed store every blob uses — through the same vault blob door. The raw rows are pruned only behind a custody latch: once the segment is provably replicated, or, on a vault that's on this device only, provably present in the local store. Reopen an archived thread and its turns rehydrate lazily from the segment, folded back in sequence and marked from-archive — read-only, because the past doesn't get rewritten. Insights never notice the difference: the digests stand in for whatever was pruned.

Manual key custody commands are in Start § back up the keys; the full offsite backup story — snapshots, the recovery kit, restore and quarantine — has its own chapter: Backups →. How requests get scoped to a vault: Devices § addressing.

§02 · the ontology

Declared once, everywhere the same.

The reason Centraid has no silos is not an integration layer — it's that there is nothing to integrate. "A person", "money moved", "something was measured" are declared exactly once, in a canonical core; every app either extends a core row (is one, plus columns) or references it (points at shared identity). No app ever re-declares a concept.

the spine

Nine load-bearing types

party · place · event · account · transaction · content_item · activity · observation · link — plus SKOS concepts for vocabulary. Identity and history live here, and nowhere else.

the domains

Thin typed extensions

Health, finance, schedule, social, knowledge, media, home, business, people, locker, tally — each adds behavior (a workout's load, an invoice's dunning), never a second identity.

the planes

Consent + agent

Scoped expiring grants, receipts and provenance on every access; agents act only through typed, precondition-checked commands. Sovereignty is a plane, not a settings page.

The model is versioned (currently ontology 1.3) and every new domain passes a seven-point admission checklist in review — anchors, links, flags, memos, search, registry, doc — so drift gets caught as process, not archaeology.

The full model is its own explorable chapter: every schema and column, the unified entity map, the ownership matrix, the gateway contract, and the ten hard rules. Read the ontology →

§03 · consent

Apps borrow. You sign. Everything is receipted.

Nothing reads or writes the vault except through the gateway, and the gateway evaluates a grant on every call. The machinery, in the order you'll meet it:

01

Install-time grants

An app's manifest declares scopes — schema, table, verbs (read, act, reveal) — and installing mints exactly that grant, idempotently. Re-publishing an app tops up only newly declared scopes; it can never silently widen what you already refused.

02

Confirm-gated commands

A handful of commands are consequential enough to pause regardless of who asks — sending a message, sending an invoice, publishing a sync batch, merging two people. When a non-owner caller invokes one, the invocation parks and waits for your decision in the blocking surface. Deny is a first-class, receipted outcome.

03

Your "no" is durable

Revoking a scope writes a tombstone: an app that later asks for it again gets blocked at the request stage, not re-granted by a sneaky update. Widening requests queue for explicit review — one open request per app, replaced on re-publish.

04

Receipts, ranked by salience

Every allow and every deny lands in the journal with who, what, why, and a risk marker. The review feed ranks by salience — denials above allows, risk-weighted — so the interesting ten of ten thousand receipts surface themselves.

Revoke a grant and the cascade is total: sessions killed, projections dark, the app's ext band retained then purged. Your rows, history and receipts remain. The gateway contract →

§04 · the outbox

The outside world gets written to only through a drawer you open.

Reads come in through staging; writes go out through the outbox. Any agent or automation that wants to touch the world — send an email, post to Slack — can only stage an artifact: the rendered thing itself, inert, sitting in a drawer with the request that would send it.

you decide

Approve · edit · discard · always-allow

You read the actual email, not a description of it. Edit it and the edited version is what's sent — atomically, in the same decision. "Always allow" mints a standing grant for exactly that (actor, verb, target) triple — this automation, sending this kind of thing, to this place — revocable any time, and still receipted on every use.

the executor sends

Credentials never touch the author

Approved items drain through a gateway-side executor that substitutes {{connection:…}} placeholders with sealed tokens at the last moment, pinned to the connection's allowed hosts over https. The automation that drafted the email never held a secret. Transient failures retry; hard failures are scrubbed and surfaced.

Sent things become rows: an email that drains binds back to a canonical social.message — your outbound acts are first-class history, not fire-and-forget JSON.

§05 · sealed columns

Some cells are ciphertext even to you.

Passwords, TOTP seeds, card numbers, OAuth tokens: columns declared sealed are AES-256-GCM ciphertext at rest, sealed inside the same write transaction that creates them. Ordinary reads get a placeholder. Getting plaintext requires the explicit reveal verb — a distinct scope apps must declare, receipted per item, so "the app looked at this password, at this time" is a fact in your journal.

The discipline is structural, not polite: sealed columns are barred from the search index by construction, journal entries carry keyed hash tokens instead of values, and error paths are scrubbed. The sealing key lives beside — not inside — the vault directory, with export, restore and rotation as receipted shell ceremonies.

Locker is the showcase: items seal their secrets, computes TOTP client-side, and its Watchtower derives weak/reused flags without ever unsealing into the transcript. Apps § blueprints →

§06 · connections & sync

The border has a customs post.

External services connect through a broker the gateway owns, and external rows enter through a staging band you review. Two mechanisms, one discipline: nothing crosses the border unattributed. This connector sync — pulling the outside world in — is not the offline device replica that keeps a phone or browser working without a connection; that lives in the Devices chapter.

a

Bring your own client

A connection is your own OAuth client (or API key) for Gmail, Google Calendar, Google Contacts, GitHub… The PKCE ceremony runs once; tokens land sealed as credential sidecar rows. The broker refreshes single-flight and injects tokens only into requests bound for the connection's pinned hosts — connector code sees {{connection:access_token}}, never the token.

b

Everything stages

Pulls land as staged batches — rows with dispositions (create / update / skip / merge-candidate) resolved against the universal external-id map, so the same Gmail thread never imports twice and upstream edits become reviewable updates. You publish a batch in one transaction; per-row provenance says exactly where each fact came from. Trusted connections can auto-publish; the vault wins conflicts either way.

c

Enrichment rides the same rails

Nine optional enrichers — photo captioning, document text extraction, face proposals, trip albums, renewal reminders and friends — derive new rows into tables the ontology already has, attributed and re-derivable, under per-class standing consent. Derived data is never a parallel store you can't audit.

Honest beta note: the broker, staging, templates and health panel are real; the guided "sign in with…" wizard UI is still landing. Today connections are configured through the automation templates.

§07 · automations

Agents on a clock, with a paper trail.

An automation is an agent with a manifest: a trigger, a grant of its own, and a durable transcript for every run. It is how data moves while you sleep — the Gmail pull, the morning briefing, the renewal nag — and it enjoys no special powers: same scopes, same typed commands, same parking for consequential acts, same receipts.

trigger

cron

Five-field schedule. The briefing at 06:30, the weekly sweep on Sundays.

trigger

webhook

A slug route with a shared-secret hash — GitHub pings it, the automation fires.

trigger

condition

A consented read on a gate: fire once per new row that matches. "A message was released."

trigger

data

The change feed itself: fire on new change entries, cursor strictly time-ordered.

Every fire runs as an enrolled agent identity — its invocations, pre/post checks and evidence land in the journal, and the run reads back as a conversation, not a log file. When you wonder why it did what it did, you read the transcript.

§08 · the assistant

The payoff: one brain over the whole record.

Silo-free data is only worth the trouble if something can think across it. The assistant is an owner-plane agent with exactly three tools — and the restraint is the design.

read

vault_sql

One read-only SELECT over the entire ontology — joins, window functions, recursive CTEs, FTS matches. Row-capped, receipted, no writes, no DDL. Your data becomes answerable because it's one schema.

act

vault_invoke

One typed command per call — the same command palette apps use. Consequential commands park for your confirmation exactly as they would for any other caller.

open

vault_content

The extracted text of one document by id — size-bounded and receipted — so "summarize the lease" reads the actual lease, not a guess.

And it learns in rows, not vibes: when you correct it, the diff is stored as a correction and distilled into a scoped judgment the agent consults before acting again — auditable, expirable, revocable like everything else here.

§09 · bytes & blobs

Own the meaning, rent the bytes.

Originals — photos, PDFs, videos up to 512 MB streamed — land in a local content-addressed store keyed by sha256, so identical bytes exist once no matter how many apps attach them. Uploading is not yet a fact: bytes stage under their hash, and only the typed command that claims the hash (add a document, add an asset) mints the canonical row and the receipt.

Derivatives — thumbnails, previews, extracted text — hang off the canonical item as attributed variants; extracted text feeds straight into search. An optional S3-compatible tier replicates the local store for durability, encrypting each blob under the vault key before it leaves — the address stays the plaintext hash, so dedup survives re-keying and the meaning never moves: lose a blob store and you've lost re-fetchable bytes, never a relationship.

With the vault Hosted, the local store becomes a bounded cache, not a second full copy: a budget (yours to set, or derived from free disk) caps the spool, tiny previews are pinned so the grid always renders, and everything else ages out least-recently-used — but nothing is ever evicted unless the remote provably holds it, and imports back-pressure rather than drop when the uplink can't keep up. Remote blobs are sealed in ranged-readable encrypted frames, compressed when the bytes allow, so scrubbing a video or opening one page fetches only the covering slices. A new device restores the vault snapshot, warms every tiny preview first — a usable library in minutes — and leaves originals to stream in on demand.

§10 · search

One index over everything the model knows.

Full-text search (SQLite FTS5) indexes the text-bearing tables across every domain — notes and their bodies, documents, messages, people, tasks, events, transactions, annotations, expenses — kept in sync by triggers in the same transaction as the write. Apps get bounded search through their scopes; the assistant gets it through SQL.

Two structural guarantees: sealed columns can't be indexed — the bar is in the schema layer, not a code-review convention — and search walks the same consent pipeline as reads, failing closed if a grant masks any indexed column.
